The Signal Journey

Right now, your phone knows where you are to within 3 meters. Here's the incredible 67-millisecond journey a GPS signal takes from space to your pocket — step by step.

1
⏱️Atomic Clock TickT = 0.000 ms
Deep inside GPS satellite IIR-M, a rubidium atomic clock ticks. It measures time by counting oscillations of rubidium-87 atoms: exactly 6,834,682,610.904 Hz. This clock loses less than 1 second in 100,000 years. Without this precision, GPS would drift 10 km per day.
⚛️ The Physics

GPS needs nanosecond timing. Light travels 30 cm per nanosecond. A 10 ns error = 3 meter position error. Each satellite carries 3-4 atomic clocks for redundancy.

🕐 Accuracy: ±10 ns📡 Frequency: 6.83 GHz💰 Clock cost: ~$50,000
2
📻Signal GenerationT = 0.001 ms
The satellite generates a navigation message: its exact position (ephemeris), the current time, atmospheric correction data, and health status. This is encoded onto two radio frequencies: L1 (1575.42 MHz) and L2 (1227.60 MHz). The signal power is just 20-50 watts — less than a light bulb.
📊 The Data

The nav message is 1,500 bits, broadcast at 50 bps. It takes 12.5 minutes to send the complete almanac of all 31 satellites. Your phone combines data from 4+ satellites simultaneously.

3
🚀Crossing the VacuumT = 0.001 → 66.7 ms
The signal leaves the satellite antenna at the speed of light: 299,792,458 m/s. It travels 20,200 km straight down through the vacuum of space. This leg takes about 67 milliseconds. The signal spreads out — by the time it reaches Earth, it's weaker than the noise from a microwave oven.
💡 Speed of Light

Distance = Speed × Time. At 299,792 km/s, the signal crosses 20,200 km in 67.38 ms. But the speed isn't constant — it changes in the atmosphere.

☽ Heritage

Ibn al-Haytham (965 CE) proved light travels in straight lines at finite speed — the very principle GPS depends on. Without his optics, we wouldn't understand signal propagation.

4
🌌Ionosphere (Electron Storm)T ≈ 66.8 ms
At 80-1,000 km altitude, the signal hits the ionosphere — a layer of electrically charged particles. Free electrons slow the signal down, adding a delay of 1-50 nanoseconds depending on solar activity. This is the #1 source of GPS error. Dual-frequency receivers (L1+L2) can cancel this out because the two frequencies are affected differently.
⚡ Ionospheric Delay

Delay is proportional to TEC / f² where TEC = Total Electron Content. Higher solar activity → more electrons → more delay → worse GPS. This is why space weather matters.

⚡ Error: 1-50 ns☀️ Worse during storms📡 Fixed by dual-freq
5
🌦️Troposphere (Weather Layer)T ≈ 67.0 ms
Below 80 km, the signal passes through the troposphere — where weather happens. Water vapor and air density cause another delay of 2-25 nanoseconds. Unlike the ionosphere, this affects both frequencies equally, so it can't be corrected with dual-frequency. Instead, mathematical models predict the delay.
💧 Wet vs Dry Delay

Dry delay (air pressure): ~2.3 m, predictable. Wet delay (humidity): 0-0.3 m, unpredictable. Rain doesn't affect it much — it's the invisible water vapor that matters.

6
🏢Multipath (Bouncing Signals)T ≈ 67.3 ms
Near the ground, the signal bounces off buildings, cars, and the ground itself. Your phone receives the direct signal plus delayed reflections. These "multipath" signals confuse the receiver, adding 1-5 meters of error in cities. This is why GPS works better in open fields than downtown.
🏙️ Urban Canyons

In dense cities, the direct signal may be blocked entirely. Your phone calculates position from reflected signals only — sometimes giving you "GPS drift" where your location jumps around.

7
📱Your Phone ReceivesT = 67.38 ms
Your phone's tiny GPS chip (smaller than a fingernail) locks onto the signal. It compares the received time code against its own clock to measure the delay. From 4+ satellites, it calculates 4 unknowns: latitude, longitude, altitude, and clock error. This is called trilateration — not triangulation.
📐 The Math

4 equations, 4 unknowns. Each satellite gives: distance = c × (t_received - t_sent). The intersection of 4 spheres gives one point in 3D space + time correction. Your phone solves this ~10 times per second.

☽ Heritage

The position calculation requires knowing Earth's radius — measured by al-Biruni in 973 CE using a mountain and trigonometry. His result (6,340 km) was accurate to within 0.5% of the modern value (6,371 km). Every GPS fix uses his measurement.

8
📍Position FixT = 67.38 ms + computation
Done. In 67 milliseconds, a signal has traveled 20,200 km from space, passed through charged plasma and weather, bounced off buildings, and told your phone where you are to within 3 meters. And it does this continuously, from 4+ satellites simultaneously, 10 times per second, for free, anywhere on Earth.
📍 Accuracy: ~3 m🛰️ Satellites used: 4-12🔄 Updates: 10/sec💰 Cost to you: $0

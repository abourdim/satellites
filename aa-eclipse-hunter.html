🌑 How It Works

1
Satellites shine by reflecting sunlight — they don't produce their own light.
2
Every orbit, the satellite passes behind Earth relative to the Sun, entering the shadow cone.
3
At shadow entry, the satellite vanishes instantly — as if someone turned off a switch.
4
At shadow exit, it reappears with a bright flash as sunlight hits the solar panels.
5
The ISS spends about 36 minutes in shadow per 92-minute orbit — 39% of each orbit is dark.

🔭 What to Watch For

The most dramatic satellite eclipses happen during evening passes when you're in darkness but the satellite is still sunlit. As the ISS crosses your sky, it will suddenly fade and disappear mid-pass — the exact moment it enters Earth's shadow.

The reverse is even more spectacular: during dawn passes, a bright dot appears from nothing as the satellite exits the shadow. It's called a "shadow exit flash".

📐 The Geometry of Shadows

Earth casts a shadow cone extending 1.4 million km into space (3.6× the Moon's distance). The umbra (total shadow) is a cone about 42,000 km wide at the Moon's distance. Every LEO satellite passes through this shadow every orbit.

The shadow isn't sharp — there's a penumbral zone where the satellite is partially lit. During penumbral transition, the satellite dims gradually over ~5 seconds before going completely dark. This gradual dimming is the signal to watch for.

⚡ Famous Satellite Eclipses

Iridium Flares + Shadow: Old Iridium satellites would produce brilliant mag -8 flares then vanish seconds later as they entered shadow — an unforgettable one-two punch.

ISS shadow transits: The ISS entering shadow during a high-elevation pass is one of the most dramatic naked-eye astronomical events. You can literally see Earth's shadow in the sky.

☽ Heritage

Ibn al-Haytham (965 CE) was the first to correctly explain how shadows work — light travels in straight lines, and opaque objects block it. His Book of Optics laid the foundation for understanding every shadow cone, every eclipse, every satellite vanishing into Earth's darkness.

The geometry of satellite eclipses is pure Ibn al-Haytham — applied at orbital scale.

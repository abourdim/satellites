📻 Radio Frequencies

The Satellite Spectrum

Every satellite whispers on a different radio frequency. GPS at 1.575 GHz, Starlink at 12 GHz, Voyager at 8.4 GHz. Here's the invisible rainbow of satellite radio — from 100 MHz to 100 GHz.

100 MHz1 GHz10 GHz100 GHz

📡 Why Different Frequencies?

Low frequencies travel far but carry little data. High frequencies carry lots of data but are blocked by rain. Every satellite mission picks the best trade-off.

🌧️ Rain Fade

Above 10 GHz, rain absorbs radio waves. Ka-band (26-40 GHz) Starlink signals weaken in storms. C-band (4-8 GHz) works in any weather — why it's used for critical comms.

⚡ Bandwidth = Speed

Higher frequency = more bandwidth = faster data. Starlink uses Ka-band for Gbps speeds. GPS uses L-band at just 50 bps — it only needs to send time and position.

🔒 Licensed vs Shared

Most satellite frequencies are licensed by the ITU (International Telecommunication Union). Amateur radio satellites share bands. Interference can jam GPS from 20,200 km away.

☽ Heritage Connection

Radio waves are light — just at wavelengths our eyes can't see. Ibn al-Haytham (965 CE) proved that light travels in straight lines, has finite speed, and obeys mathematical laws. Every satellite radio link is his optics applied at wavelengths he never imagined — from 3 meters (VHF) to 3 millimeters (Ka-band).

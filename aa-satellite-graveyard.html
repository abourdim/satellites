Three Ways to Die

When a satellite reaches end-of-life, there are only three options. The choice depends on altitude.

🔥

Controlled De-orbit

LEO satellites (200–2,000 km)

Fire thrusters to lower the orbit. The satellite hits the upper atmosphere at 28,000 km/h, heating to 1,500°C. Most burns up. Surviving pieces target Point Nemo — the most remote spot in the Pacific Ocean.

The ISS will be de-orbited this way around 2031. It will be the largest object ever to re-enter.

👻

Graveyard Orbit

GEO satellites (~35,786 km)

GEO satellites are too high to de-orbit (it would take too much fuel). Instead, they boost 300 km higher into a "graveyard orbit" — a junkyard ring above the Clarke Belt. They'll drift there for millions of years.

~300 dead satellites already orbit in this ghostly ring, silent and tumbling in the dark.

🎲

Uncontrolled Decay

Failed or abandoned satellites

If a satellite dies before it can de-orbit, atmospheric drag slowly pulls it down over months to centuries. It eventually burns up — but nobody controls where the debris falls. In 2022, SpaceX lost 40 Starlinks to a solar storm.

Some dead satellites will orbit for 10,000+ years before finally decaying.

Point Nemo — The Spacecraft Cemetery

The most remote point on Earth, chosen specifically as a graveyard for satellites and space stations.

🌊 Oceanic Pole of Inaccessibility

48°52.6′S 123°23.6′W — South Pacific Ocean

Point Nemo is 2,688 km from the nearest land in every direction. The closest humans are often the astronauts on the ISS, 408 km overhead. It is the safest place on Earth to crash a spacecraft.

Since 1971, over 263 spacecraft have been targeted here — including Russia's Mir station (2001), ESA's ATV cargo vehicles, and dozens of Progress supply ships. The ISS will join them around 2031.

The Ghost Gallery

Famous dead satellites still orbiting — silent, tumbling, unreachable.

🛰️

Vanguard 1

Launched 1958 · Dead since 1964

The oldest human object in orbit. 1.5 kg. Will orbit for 240+ years. Still up there, silent, circling.

📡

Envisat

Died 2012 · 8 tons

Europe's largest Earth observation sat. Died suddenly. Too big to de-orbit. A collision risk for centuries.

🔭

IRAS

Died 1983 · Infrared telescope

First infrared space telescope. Nearly collided with a dead satellite in 2020 — missing by 47 meters.

⚡

Fengyun-1C Debris

Destroyed 2007 · 3,500+ pieces

China's ASAT test. Still the largest debris cloud in LEO. Will persist for decades.

📻

Cosmos 954

Crashed 1978 · Nuclear reactor

Soviet nuclear-powered sat crashed in northern Canada. Spread radioactive debris across 124,000 km².

🇺🇸

Skylab

Crashed 1979 · 77 tons

America's first space station. Re-entered over Australia. The town of Esperance fined NASA $400 for littering.

☽ Heritage Reflection

For a thousand years, the sky was pristine. Al-Sufi (964 CE) cataloged 1,018 stars against a perfectly clear firmament. Ulugh Beg (1420 CE) measured their positions with naked-eye precision that wouldn't be exceeded for centuries.

Today, 36,500+ objects orbit overhead. Astronomers struggle to photograph through the Starlink trains. The sky that inspired al-Battani, Ibn al-Haytham, and al-Zarqali is being transformed by the technology their work made possible.

The satellite graveyard raises a question the medieval astronomers never imagined: who is responsible for keeping the sky clean?

"The sky that inspired a thousand years of science is being filled by the science it inspired."

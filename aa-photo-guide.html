📸 Photography

Night Sky Photo Guide

Capture satellites streaking across the sky, ISS passes, Starlink trains, and star trails. From phone cameras to DSLRs — here's everything you need.

📱 Phone Photography

Modern phones can capture surprisingly good night sky photos. The key is a Night Mode or Pro/Manual mode that allows long exposures.

Mode
Night/Pro
ISO
800-3200
Exposure
10-30 sec

Essential: Use a tripod or prop phone against a solid surface. Any movement during the exposure ruins the shot.

💡Set a 3-second timer to avoid shake when pressing the shutter button.

📱 Best Phone Features

iPhone: Night mode auto-activates in low light. Use a 10-30 sec exposure on a tripod. Stars will appear.

Android: Use Pro mode. Set ISO 1600, shutter 15 sec, manual focus to infinity.

Pixel: Astrophotography mode activates on tripod. Takes 4-minute composite. Excellent results.

📷 DSLR / Mirrorless Setup

A dedicated camera with manual controls produces dramatically better results. Here are optimal starting settings:

Aperture
f/2.8
ISO
1600-6400
Shutter
15-30 sec

Lens: Wide angle (14-24mm) is ideal. Faster aperture (f/1.4-2.8) captures more light. Kit lens (f/3.5) works but needs higher ISO.

📐500 Rule: Max shutter (sec) = 500 ÷ focal length. At 24mm: 500÷24 = 20 sec max before stars trail.

⭐ Star Trails

Want circular star trails? Point at Polaris and expose for 30+ minutes. Or stack many 30-second exposures.

Satellite bonus: Satellites create straight lines through your circular star trails — a dramatic contrast showing human technology against the ancient sky.

🛰️ Photographing Satellites

Satellites appear as bright dots moving steadily across the sky. To capture them:

Exposure
15-60 sec
Result
Bright streak
Best time
Dusk/dawn

A 30-second exposure turns a satellite pass into a bright line across the frame. The ISS creates the brightest trails — brighter than any star.

📶 Starlink Trains

Freshly launched Starlink satellites travel in a "pearl string" formation. Multiple parallel lines in one frame. Use 10-15 sec exposure to catch several dots in a row. Most visible 1-3 weeks after launch before they spread out.

🏗️ ISS Pass Photography

The ISS is magnitude -4 to -6 — brighter than Venus. Creates the most spectacular trails.

Technique: Set camera to continuous 30-sec exposures. Stack all images where ISS is visible. Result: one bright arc across the sky with star points.

🌟ISS moves ~1° per second. In a 30-sec exposure, it crosses ~30° of sky — a long, bright streak.

📜 Al-Zarqali — Master of the Astrolabe

Abū Isḥāq Ibrāhīm al-Zarqālī (1029–1087 CE) of Toledo, Al-Andalus. He invented the universal astrolabe — the first to work at any latitude, not just one. Previous astrolabes had a separate tympan plate for each city. Al-Zarqali's innovation made a single instrument universal.

He also corrected the orbit of Mercury — determining it was an ellipse, not a circle — 500 years before Kepler. His Toledo Tables were the most accurate astronomical tables for two centuries and were used across Europe.
"The astrolabe was the smartphone of the medieval world — calculator, clock, compass, calendar, and star chart in one brass disc."

🔧 How the Astrolabe Works

1
The tympan (back plate) shows altitude and azimuth circles for your latitude — the fixed coordinate grid of your sky.
2
The rete (star map overlay) is a cutwork brass plate showing major stars. It rotates to represent the sky turning throughout the day.
3
The alidade (ruler on the back) measures the altitude of a star or the Sun by sighting along it.
4
Rotate the rete until the measured star sits on the correct altitude circle. The rule then reads the time from the hour scale.
5
To predict when a star rises or sets, rotate the rete until it touches the horizon line — the same principle as SGP4 pass prediction.
"The astrolabe is the original satellite tracker."

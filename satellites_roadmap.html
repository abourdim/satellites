01 — Vision

What We're Building

Not a satellite tracker. Not a toy. A beautiful, real instrument that reveals the invisible architecture of space above your head — powered by live orbital data.

🌌

The Night Sky, Not a Map

Look up, not down. A fullscreen starfield — real constellations, real positions, invisible satellites revealed as glowing dots crossing silently overhead. Use your phone's gyroscope as a magic window to space.

📡

Real Data, Not Fiction

Every dot on screen is a real satellite at its real position in its real orbit. Live TLE data from CelesTrak, propagated in real time using SGP4. Actual names, missions, altitudes, orbital periods.

🧠

Progressive Depth

A 4-year-old sees glowing dots and learns "that's the Space Station — 6 people live there." A 14-year-old parses Keplerian elements and calculates orbital decay. Same sky, four levels of depth.

🕌

Islamic Heritage — Woven In

Islamic astronomers mapped the stars that satellites now use for orientation. Arabic names fill the sky. Al-Sufi's star catalog, Al-Biruni's Earth, Al-Tusi's orbital math — not a museum tab, a living presence.

04 — Islamic Astronomy Heritage

The Roots of Space

Every satellite overhead exists because humans first learned to read the sky. For centuries, Islamic scholars mapped the stars, calculated orbits, and built instruments of breathtaking precision.

903 – 986 AD

Al-Sufi

Wrote the Book of Fixed Stars — the most accurate star catalog of the medieval world. First human to record the Andromeda Galaxy. Modern satellite star-trackers recognize the same patterns he mapped.

780 – 850 AD

Al-Khwarizmi

Father of algebra and algorithms. The SGP4 algorithm that calculates every satellite position in real time runs on his mathematical legacy. The word "algorithm" comes from his name.

965 – 1040 AD

Ibn al-Haytham

Father of optics. Explained how light travels, how lenses work, how the eye sees. Every telescope, every satellite camera, every optical sensor descends from his Book of Optics.

973 – 1048 AD

Al-Biruni

Calculated Earth's radius to within 16 km of the true value using a mountain and trigonometry. Satellites need Earth's exact shape to maintain orbit. Also proposed Earth rotates on its axis.

858 – 929 AD

Al-Battani

Refined the length of the year to within 2 minutes of the true value. Improved solar and lunar orbit calculations. His trigonometric methods predict satellite passes and eclipses today.

1201 – 1274 AD

Al-Tusi

Created the "Tusi couple" — resolving circular into linear motion. Influenced Copernicus directly. Built the Maragheh Observatory. His work connects to understanding orbital mechanics.

1394 – 1449 AD

Ulugh Beg

Built the Samarkand Observatory and produced a catalog of 1,018 stars with precision unmatched for 150 years. His catalog is the ancestor of digital star databases loaded into satellites today.

1029 – 1087 AD

Al-Zarqali

Built the most advanced universal astrolabe — working at any latitude. Corrected Ptolemy's orbital models. The astrolabe was essentially an analog satellite tracker computing celestial positions in real time.

810 – 887 AD

Abbas ibn Firnas

First human flight attempt in Córdoba. Built a planetarium with mechanical planets and simulated weather. His dream of flight was the first step on the path from gliding to orbit.

05 — Genius Feature Ideas

Beyond the Roadmap

Ideas that could make this unforgettable. Each one a potential future feature — prioritized by impact and feasibility.

Live Physics Lab Physics

Real-time orbital mechanics on each satellite: gravitational pull, centripetal acceleration, orbital velocity. "This satellite travels at 7.66 km/s — why doesn't it fly away?" Interactive force diagrams.

Live Math Problems Math

"The ISS is 420 km up, moving at 27,600 km/h. How long is one orbit?" Kid calculates, app verifies with live data. Trigonometry, circular motion, real orbital math from real objects.

Time Machine Mode Time

Slider from 1957 (Sputnik, 1 object) → today (10,000+). Watch the sky fill decade by decade. The Cold War. The GPS era. The Starlink explosion. Feel how fast humanity filled space.

Orbital Music Sound

Each satellite plays a tone based on altitude. LEO = low hum, MEO = mid, GEO = sustained high. ISS gets its own melody. Starlink trains become a rising scale. Orbits as ambient music.

Zoom Out to Orbits Data

Pinch to zoom: your sky → your city → Earth from space → full orbital shells. See the thin LEO ring, the GPS constellation at MEO, the GEO belt as a perfect ring. Understand why "space is crowded."

Culture Cards Culture

Satellite launched from Kazakhstan? → Baikonur history, Kazakh culture. From French Guiana? → Why Europe launches from South America. Every satellite is a geography and geopolitics lesson.

Spot the Satellite — AR Guide AR

Turns screen time into sky time.

  • App calculates exactly when a satellite becomes visible to the naked eye
  • Points you to the right part of the sky: "Look northwest, 40° up, in 3 minutes"
  • Countdown timer, then: "THERE — did you see it?" — verification with real data
  • ISS spotting challenge with notification alerts before passes

Gravity Simulator Math

Drop a ball at different altitudes. At ground level, it falls. At 200 km, it falls and misses Earth — orbit. Too slow = crash, too fast = escape. Newton's cannonball, made interactive.

My Sky Journal Data

Auto-generated nightly summaries. Monthly reports. Personal records ("rarest satellite you've spotted"). A real dataset about your sky over time. Export for school science projects.

Satellite Graveyard Culture

Where do old satellites go? The graveyard orbit 300 km above GEO. Point Nemo — the spacecraft cemetery in the Pacific. How many dead satellites are still up there drifting forever.

Design Your Orbit Math

Pick altitude and inclination. App calculates period, speed, ground track. "Want it to pass over your house daily? Here's the orbit." Compare to real missions. Learn why polar orbits see everything.

The Altitude Ladder Physics

Everything to scale in one vertical visualization: your head (1.5m) → birds (300m) → clouds (2 km) → planes (11 km) → edge of space (100 km) → ISS (420 km) → GPS (20,200 km) → GEO (35,786 km) → Moon (384,400 km). A child suddenly grasps how thin the atmosphere is — and how far "space" really stretches.

Launch Countdown Time

"In 4 hours, a Falcon 9 launches from Cape Canaveral carrying 22 Starlink satellites." Anticipation. Then verification — tomorrow, new dots appear in your sky. Real-time space industry.

Eclipse Hunter Physics

Satellites pass through Earth's shadow every orbit. Predict the exact second one vanishes and reappears with a flash. "Watch the ISS — it will disappear in 12 seconds." Dramatic and verifiable.

Space Junk Awareness Culture

23,000+ tracked debris pieces. A fleck of paint at orbital speed hits like a bullet. Visualize collision risk. Kessler Syndrome — the chain reaction that could close space. The environmental message for orbit.

Astrolabe vs Star-Tracker Culture

Interactive comparison: use a virtual astrolabe to find your latitude, then watch a satellite star-tracker do it instantly. Same principle, 1000 years apart. Islamic innovation meets space tech.

06 — Technical Foundation

Tech Stack & APIs

Built to be lightweight, fast, and accessible. Single HTML file for Phase 1, progressive enhancement for later phases.

Frontend

Single HTML/CSS/JS file. Canvas rendering for starfield and satellites. CSS animations for UI. No frameworks — pure, fast, portable. Progressive Web App (PWA) ready.

Satellite Data

CelesTrak API — free TLE (Two-Line Element) data for 10,000+ objects. Space-Track.org for deeper catalog access. Updated multiple times daily.

Orbit Propagation

SGP4 algorithm (satellite.js library). Calculates real-time position from TLE data client-side. No server needed. Sub-kilometer accuracy for LEO objects.

Location & Orientation

Browser Geolocation API for GPS. Device Orientation API for gyroscope/compass magic window. Manual city picker fallback. Horizon calculation for visible passes.

Star Catalog

Bright Star Catalogue (Yale BSC) for accurate star positions. Constellation line data. Arabic name database. Renders real sky matching your location and time.

Audio

Web Audio API for ambient space sounds, altitude-based tones, and pass alerts. All audio optional and togglable. Spatial audio for directional awareness.
